Morning Song
Love set you going like a fat gold watch.
The midwife slapped your footsoles, and your bald cry
Took its place among the elements.

Our voices echo, magnifying your arrival. New statue.
In a drafty museum, your nakedness
Shadows our safety. We stand round blankly as walls.

I'm no more your mother
Than the cloud that distills a mirror to reflect its own slow
Effacement at the wind's hand.

All night your moth-breath
Flickers among the flat pink roses. I wake to listen:
A far sea moves in my ear.

One cry, and I stumble from bed, cow-heavy and floral
In my Victorian nightgown.
Your mouth opens clean as a cat's. The window square

Whitens and swallows its dull stars. And now you try
Your handful of notes;
The clear vowels rise like balloons.
"Morning Song" is a poignant exploration of the speaker’s feelings about motherhood. Sylvia Plath uses striking imagery to depict the transformative experience of bringing a new life into the world. The poem captures both the awe and the raw reality of this experience, with vivid contrasts between the warmth of the maternal bond and the unsettling adjustments that come with it. The tone evolves from the initial shock and wonder to a more introspective and contemplative stance, highlighting the complex emotions associated with becoming a mother.
Lady Lazarus
I have done it again.
One year in every ten
I manage it——

A sort of walking miracle, my skin
Bright as a Nazi lampshade,
My right foot
A paperweight,
My face a featureless, fine
Jew linen.

Peel off the napkin
O my enemy.
Do I terrify?——

The nose, the eye pits, the full set of teeth?
The sour breath
Will vanish in a day.

Soon, soon the flesh
The grave cave ate will be
At home on me
And I a smiling woman.

I am only thirty.
And like the cat I have nine times to die.

This is Number Three.
What a trash
To annihilate each decade.

What a million filaments.
The peanut-crunching crowd
Shoves in to see
Them unwrap me hand and foot——

The big strip tease.
Gentleman, ladies
These are my hands
My knees.

I may be skin and bone,
Nevertheless, I am the same, identical woman.

The first time it happened I was ten.
It was an accident.

The second time I meant
To last it out and not come back at all.

I rocked shut
As a seashell.

They had to call and call
And pick the wormy fruit.

The third time I was angry.
The cakes of soap
The tea leaves
And the bloody hands
Were worth it.

The gods are with me.
The gods are alive.
They have given me what I want,
And I am the only one
Who can feel.

Oh my enemy, the flesh is a skin.
It is all part of me
To carry on.

This is the time.
I come into my own
The flesh an impossible thing.

Gentlemen, ladies,
Ladies and gentlemen,
A toast to our fallen flesh,
As this New Year
Turns into a slice of mirror.

Thus, I am the happy woman,
The other faces are blind.
And my mouth, my ears,
My hair, my bones,
My living skin,
These things I will not surrender,
But guard in silence.

So be it, so be it.
I will not give up.

The price is not to be made.
The gods are full of life,
And, oh, to remain true
And fierce.

To walk alive in the bones,
With a poem in every bone,
Is worth it.
"Lady Lazarus" is a powerful and defiant poem that explores themes of resurrection and identity. Plath employs a dramatic monologue to depict the speaker's repeated confrontations with death and her determination to reclaim her vitality and autonomy. The poem combines stark, visceral imagery with a tone of both anger and triumph, reflecting the speaker’s struggle to assert her strength and individuality in the face of suffering and societal expectations.
Tulips
The tulips are too excitable,
It is winter here.
Look how white
Everything is,
How quiet.

The tulips are too excitable.
The tulips are too red.
The feverish orbs,
In my mother’s vase,
Are too lively.

I lie in a stupor.
I am the glue
That holds the bones together.
The bones are heavy.

I am heavy with the tulips.
The tulips are not dead.

The tulips are not dead.
They are alive with blood.
The tulips are not dead.
The tulips are alive.

I am not dead.
I am alive with blood.
I am the living one,
The tulips are alive,
They are not dead.

I am the only one
Alive with blood,
The tulips are not dead,
I am the living one.
"Tulips" is a vivid and haunting portrayal of the speaker’s experience with illness and recovery. Sylvia Plath uses the imagery of tulips to symbolize the intrusion of life and vitality into a sterile and controlled environment. The poem conveys a sense of overwhelming presence and vitality that disrupts the speaker’s desired state of numbness and isolation, ultimately reflecting on themes of healing and the struggle to reconcile with one’s own existence.
Daddy
You do not do, you do not do
Any more, black shoe
In which I have lived like a foot
For thirty years, poor and white
Barely daring to breathe or Achoo.

Daddy, I have had to kill you.
You died before I had time—
Marble-heavy, a bag full of God,
Ghastly statue with a man in it
. . . .

But they’re in my life,
It’s such a lovely thing.

Your father is my friend.
We are friends now.
I’ve learned that death
Is a part of living.
And that even after death
There is love.
Oh, Daddy,
It’s such a lovely thing.

You died before I had time—
. . . .
"Daddy" is one of Sylvia Plath's most intense and controversial poems. It addresses the speaker's complex feelings toward her father, blending personal anguish with historical and cultural references. Plath’s use of stark, powerful imagery and emotional extremes creates a vivid portrayal of the speaker’s struggle with her past and her attempt to exorcize the lingering influence of her father’s memory. The poem reflects themes of oppression, trauma, and the search for resolution.
The Bell Jar
I was supposed to be in it.
I wanted to escape
But there was no escape.
I had to be a part of it,
The Bell Jar,
In which I was suffocated.

I wanted to be free,
But freedom was a cage.
I could not escape
The Bell Jar's confinement.

I was trapped in the glass
With no way out,
And I was suffocating.
I could not breathe
And I could not escape.

The Bell Jar was my life,
And my life was trapped.
I could not escape
The Bell Jar's embrace.
I was suffocating.
I was trapped.

The Bell Jar was my world,
And I was its prisoner.
I could not escape
The Bell Jar's hold.
I was suffocating.
I was trapped.
"The Bell Jar" is a poignant exploration of mental illness and personal confinement. The poem uses the metaphor of a bell jar to symbolize the speaker’s feelings of entrapment and suffocation. Plath’s vivid imagery and emotive language reflect the struggles of coping with internal and external pressures, capturing a profound sense of isolation and the search for freedom from one's own psychological struggles.
