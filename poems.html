Daddy
You do not do, you do not doIndicates the speaker’s emotional struggle with her father’s memory.
Any more, black shoe
In which I have lived like a foot
For thirty years, poor and white,
Barely daring to breathe or Achoo.

Daddy, I have had to kill you.Represents the speaker’s need to overcome and let go of the dominating presence of her father.
You died before I had time—
Marble-heavy, a bag full of God,
Ghastly statue with a cement eye.

I could hardly speak.Expresses the speaker’s sense of being silenced or oppressed.
I thought every German was you.
And the language obscene
An engine, an engine
Chuffing me off like a Jew.

The bruteSymbolizes the harsh and oppressive force of the father’s memory.
I used to pray to recover you.

The black manRepresents a destructive force, possibly the speaker’s own feelings of rage or self-hatred.
With his rope around your neck.
I have always been scared of you.
And your cute, cute, cute
Just like a mother with the exact same face.

"Daddy" is a powerful and intense poem where Plath deals with themes of oppression, loss, and liberation. The speaker’s complex feelings toward her father are expressed through striking and often unsettling imagery, reflecting both personal trauma and a desire to break free from its constraints.
Ariel
This is the lightRepresents potential and possibility.
From what I could be,
But what a fool I am!

Tying my tongueSymbolizes a struggle to express oneself.
In speechless
Plenitude.

With it, the world moves,
The wide smile.
I am the myth.

Groping toward the moonSymbolizes the pursuit of enlightenment or truth.
An armful of purple flowers.
An armful of white.

I am the firstRepresents a sense of uniqueness and new beginnings.
Between a flowering and the sea.
Glimmering, glimmering.

This is the way of the world,
In mid-flight
As I wake from my dream.
I am the sword in the hand.
I am the light that transforms.
"Ariel" is a vibrant and dynamic poem in which Plath explores themes of transformation and liberation. Through a series of vivid and often surreal images, the speaker undergoes a powerful metamorphosis, moving from a state of confinement to one of freedom and transcendence.
Lady Lazarus
I have done it again.The speaker refers to a repetitive act of resurrection or survival.
One year in every ten
I manage it——
A sort of walking miracle,
My skin bright as a Nazi lampshade,

My right footSymbolizes the speaker's sense of identity and individual struggle.
A paperweight,
My life is a novel,
A little used book in the attic
Of the house of the world.

I riseRepresents the act of overcoming adversity.
Out of the ash
I rise
With my red hair
And I eat men like air.

Tulips
The tulips are too excitable, it is winter here.Describes the contrast between the speaker’s desired peaceful state and the disruptive presence of the tulips.
Look how white everything is, how quiet, how snowed-in.
I am learning peacefulness, lying by myself quietly
As the light lies on these white walls, this bed, these hands.
I am nobody; I have nothing to do with explosions.
I have given my name and my day-clothes up to the nurses
And my history to the anesthetist and my body to surgeons.

They have propped my head between the pillow and the sheet-cuffRepresents the speaker’s physical and emotional confinement.
Like an eye between two white lids that will not shut.
Stupid pupil, it has to take everything in.
The nurses pass and pass, they are no trouble,
They pass the way gulls pass inland in their white caps,
Doing things with their hands, one just the same as another,
So it is impossible to tell how many there are.

My body is a pebble to them, they tend it as waterDescribes the speaker’s sense of being just another object to the medical staff.
Tends to the pebbles it must run over, smoothing them gently.
They bring me numbness in their bright needles, they bring me sleep.
Now I have lost myself I am sick of baggage——
My patent leather overnight case like a black pillbox,
My husband and child smiling out of the family photo;
Their smiles catch onto my skin, little smiling hooks.

I have let things slip, a thirty-year-old cargo boatRepresents the speaker’s sense of losing control and the burden of personal history.
stubbornly hanging on to my name and address.
They have swabbed me clear of my loving associations.
Scared and bare on the green plastic-pillowed trolley
I watched my teaset, my bureaus of linen, my books
Sink out of sight, and the water went over my head.
I am a nun now, I have never been so pure.

I didn’t want any flowers,Indicates the speaker’s desire for emptiness and peace, rejecting external distractions.
I only wanted
To lie with my hands turned up and be utterly empty.
How free it is, you have no idea how free——
The peacefulness is so big it dazes you,
And it asks nothing, a name tag, a few trinkets.
It is what the dead close on, finally; I imagine them
Shutting their mouths on it, like a Communion tablet.

The tulips are too redRepresents the disruptive and jarring nature of the tulips against the speaker’s desired tranquility.
In the first place, they hurt me.
Even through the gift paper I could hear them breathe
Lightly, through their white swaddlings, like an awful baby.
Their redness talks to my wound, it corresponds.
They are subtle : they seem to float, though they weigh me down,
Upsetting me with their sudden tongues and their color,
A dozen red lead sinkers round my neck.

Nobody watched me before,The tulips are seen as a symbol of intrusion and unwanted attention.
Now I am watched.
The tulips turn to me, and the window behind me
Where once a day the light slowly widens and slowly thins,
And I see myself, flat, ridiculous, a cut-paper shadow
Between the eye of the sun and the eyes of the tulips,
And I have no face, I have wanted to efface myself.
The vivid tulips eat my oxygen.

Before they cameDescribes the peaceful state before the tulips' intrusion.
The air was calm enough,
Coming and going, breath by breath, without any fuss.
Then the tulips filled it up like a loud noise.
Now the air snags and eddies round them the way a river
Snags and eddies round a sunken rust-red engine.
They concentrate my attention, that was happy
Playing and resting without committing itself.

The walls, also, seem to be warming themselves.The walls reflect the speaker's growing awareness of the intrusion of the tulips.
The tulips should be behind bars like dangerous animals;
They are opening like the mouth of some great African cat,
And I am aware of my heart: it opens and closes
Its bowl of red blooms out of sheer love of me.
The water I taste is warm and salt, like the sea,
And comes from a country far away as health.
"Tulips" reflects a confrontation between the speaker’s desire for peace and the disruptive force of external stimuli. The tulips represent a jarring intrusion into the speaker's state of tranquility, leading to a complex interplay between inner and outer worlds.
Morning Song
Love set you going like a fat gold watch.The opening line uses the metaphor of a watch to symbolize the speaker’s affection and the beginning of a new life.
The midwife slapped your footsoles, and your bald cry
Took its place among the elements.

Our voices echo, magnifying your arrival.The speaker's joy and the significance of the newborn’s arrival are emphasized.
New statue.
In a drafty museum, your nakedness
Shadows our safety. We stand round blankly as walls.

I’m no more your motherRepresents the speaker’s feeling of detachment and disconnection from traditional maternal roles.
Than the cloud that distills a mirror to reflect its own slow
Effacement at the wind’s hand.

All night your moth-breathSymbolizes the fragile and delicate nature of the newborn’s presence.
Flickers among the flat pink roses. I wake to listen:
A far sea moves in my ear.

One cry, and I stumble from bed, cow-heavy and floralDescribes the speaker’s clumsiness and disorientation in response to the baby’s cry.
In my Victorian nightgown.
Your mouth opens clean as a cat’s. The window square

Whitens and swallows its dull stars.Represents the transition from night to morning, symbolizing the new beginning with the baby.
And now you try
Your handful of notes;
The clear vowels rise like balloons.
"Morning Song" celebrates the arrival of a new baby and the transformative power of new life. Through vivid imagery and metaphors, Plath captures the wonder and adjustment of becoming a mother, reflecting on the changes brought about by the newborn’s presence.
