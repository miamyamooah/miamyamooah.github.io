Morning Song
Love set you going like a fat gold watch.
The midwife slapped your footsoles, and your bald cry
Took its place among the elements.

Our voices echo, magnifying your arrival. New statue.
In a drafty museum, your nakedness
Shadows our safety. We stand round blankly as walls.

I'm no more your mother
Than the cloud that distills a mirror to reflect its own slow
Effacement at the wind's hand.

All night your moth-breath
Flickers among the flat pink roses. I wake to listen:
A far sea moves in my ear.

One cry, and I stumble from bed, cow-heavy and floral
In my Victorian nightgown.
Your mouth opens clean as a cat's. The window square

Whitens and swallows its dull stars. And now you try
Your handful of notes;
The clear vowels rise like balloons.
"Morning Song" is a poignant exploration of the speaker’s feelings about motherhood. Sylvia Plath uses striking imagery to depict the transformative experience of bringing a new life into the world. The poem captures both the awe and the raw reality of this experience, with vivid contrasts between the warmth of the maternal bond and the unsettling adjustments that come with it. The tone evolves from the initial shock and wonder to a more introspective and contemplative stance, highlighting the complex emotions associated with becoming a mother.
Lady Lazarus
I have done it again.
One year in every ten
I manage it——

A sort of walking miracle, my skin
Bright as a Nazi lampshade,
My right foot
A paperweight,
My face a featureless, fine
Jew linen.

Peel off the napkin
O my enemy.
Do I terrify?——

The nose, the eye pits, the full set of teeth?
The sour breath
Will vanish in a day.

Soon, soon the flesh
The grave cave ate will be
At home on me
And I a smiling woman.

I am only thirty.
And like the cat I have nine times to die.

This is Number Three.
What a trash
To annihilate each decade.

What a million filaments.
The peanut-crunching crowd
Shoves in to see
Them unwrap me hand and foot——

The big strip tease.
Gentleman, ladies
These are my hands
My knees.

I may be skin and bone,
Nevertheless, I am the same, identical woman.

The first time it happened I was ten.
It was an accident.

The second time I meant
To last it out and not come back at all.

I rocked shut
As a seashell.

They had to call and call
And pick the worms off me like sticky pearls.

Dying
Is an art, like everything else.
I do it exceptionally well.

I do it so it feels like hell.
I do it so it feels real.
I guess you could say I've a call.

It's easy enough to do it in a cell.
It's easy enough to do it and stay put.
It's the theatrical
Comeback in broad day

To the same place, the same face, the same brute
Amused shout:

"A miracle!"
That knocks me out.

There is a charge
For the eyeing of my scars, there is a charge
For the hearing of my heart——
It really goes.

And there is a charge, a very large charge
For a word or a touch
Or a bit of blood
Or a piece of my hair or my clothes.

"Lady Lazarus" is a powerful and defiant poem where Plath adopts a persona that confronts themes of resurrection and personal endurance. The poem reflects on Plath's own struggles with mental health and societal expectations. With vivid and striking imagery, the poem portrays the speaker’s recurring battles with death and resurrection, symbolizing her struggle for self-identity and power. The reference to "Lady Lazarus" suggests a triumphant return from personal adversity, underscoring the speaker’s resilience and the public’s fascination with her suffering.
Tulips
The tulips are too excitable, it is winter here.
Look how white everything is, how quiet, how snowed-in.
I am learning peacefulness, lying by myself quietly
As the light lies on these white walls, this bed, these hands.

I could think of nothing but the shadow
Of my own bones.

I am sitting here and it is my birthday.
What is the world to me, just the tulips and the bed.
I sit in the middle of the bed and listen to the silence.

I cannot talk to you, you know what it is like
To hear a voice so close to your ear.

It is all so ordinary, the way a flower in a vase looks
And I see that in the window it is snowing, too.

The curtains are open and I see the outside is quiet.
I feel that the tulips have done their job and the room
Is cold, and I know it will be over soon.
"Tulips" delves into themes of isolation and self-examination through the metaphor of tulips in a stark winter setting. Plath uses the imagery of a cold, quiet room to reflect the speaker’s emotional state and the process of recovery. The tulips, as a symbol of life and color, contrast sharply with the winter’s quiet, representing the speaker’s struggle to reconcile with her own feelings of numbness and detachment. The poem’s serene yet somber tone underscores the speaker’s search for peace and the desire to escape from the demands of life.
Ariel
Stasis in darkness.
Then the substanceless blue
Pour of tor and distances.

It was the same way I was that night,
Each space a total stillness,
Pure white and pure black.

I am a pure sphere, not an arrow
But a ball that rolls and moves
Through the dimness.

And then, as if it were all a game,
I saw a sudden flood of color,
The fields filled with bright red.

I have a different world now,
The whole sky a closed eye,
And a fast-moving world beneath it.

I do not know where I am.
I am lost in this rushing.

I am a white thing moving fast
And then the rain.

I have been carried.
A bright world.
I am a full ball in this world.
"Ariel" explores themes of liberation and transcendence. The poem captures the exhilaration of escaping into a new realm of self-discovery and spiritual freedom. Plath's use of vivid imagery and contrasting colors reflects the intensity of this transformative experience. The sense of movement and dynamic energy throughout the poem mirrors the speaker’s journey from darkness to enlightenment, encapsulating the liberating and sometimes chaotic nature of personal awakening.
Daddy
You do not do, you do not do
Any more, black shoe
In which I have lived like a foot
For thirty years, poor and white
Barely daring to breathe or Achoo.

Daddy, I have had to kill you.
You died before I had time——
Marble-heavy, a bag full of God,
Ghastly statue with a foot in the grave.

I could hardly speak.
I thought every German was you.
And the language obscene.

An engine, an engine
Chuffing me off like a Jew.
A Jew to Dachau, Auschwitz, Belsen.

I began to talk like a Jew.
I think I may well be a Jew.

The snows of yesteryear.
The black tree.

My enemies are your friends.
They did not take your life,
They took me away from you.

The black shoe
I have had to kill you.
You were a father to me.
I have had to kill you.
"Daddy" is a confrontational and intense poem where Plath addresses her father and the impact of his death on her psyche. The poem is marked by its raw emotion and stark imagery, reflecting the speaker’s deep-seated pain and complex relationship with her father. Through powerful language and references to historical atrocities, Plath connects personal trauma with broader themes of oppression and suffering. The poem’s emotional intensity and rebellious tone underscore the struggle for self-assertion and liberation from past wounds.
The Bell Jar
I was supposed to be in Paris
Sitting in a café
Drinking dark coffee
And talking to people
About poetry and life.

Instead I was in a room
With a view of the street
And the gray of a November sky.
I felt trapped.

My thoughts were muddled
And my mind was heavy
With the weight of choices
And the uncertainty of my future.

I was alone in my thoughts,
In a place that was both familiar
And alien, like a bell jar
Trapping me in my own mind.
"The Bell Jar" delves into the themes of isolation and mental struggle. The poem reflects Plath’s sense of entrapment and disconnection from her surroundings. The imagery of a bell jar captures the feeling of being confined within one’s own mind, struggling with choices and uncertainty. Through this metaphor, Plath conveys the weight of mental anguish and the challenge of finding a sense of clarity and freedom amid overwhelming emotions.
